What is Procurement in Project Management?

Procurement refers to the gathering of external resources through purchase, rental, or contracting to achieve project objectives. Just like any other aspect of the project, these associations require effective management. Efficiently overseeing these relationships involves optimizing the quality derived from external vendors enlisted by the company to support its operations. Constraints in vendor management are primarily centered on cost and time considerations. Procurement management serves as a means to enhance the efficiency and productivity of the entire process, encompassing sourcing, requisitioning, ordering, expediting, inspection, and reconciliation of procurement. How do we control this entire procedure? One way is to create a procurement management plan.

Procurement Management Plans

A procurement management plan is like a roadmap for manufacturing companies. It helps them get the materials and supplies they need for making things. This plan covers everything from finding suppliers to making deals with them. Having a good plan ensures that all the materials arrive on time and without any hiccups. In today's competitive market, having a solid plan gives a manufacturing company an advantage over others. By planning and buying things carefully, the company can get the best prices for what they need and avoid delays that might mess up their schedule.

Parts of a Procurement Management Plan

  1. Set Procurement Goals and Plans: Begin by clearly defining what your organization aims to achieve through procuring goods and services. Outline the overall strategy to reach these goals.
  2. Spot Possible Suppliers: Once the objectives and strategies are in place, start looking for potential suppliers who can provide the necessary goods or services to meet your goals.
  3. Evaluate Suppliers: Evaluate the identified suppliers to determine which one suits your organization best. Consider factors such as price, quality, and delivery times during this evaluation.
  4. Pick a Supplier: After the evaluation process, select the supplier that aligns most closely with your organization's needs. This involves finalizing a contract with them.
  5. Keep an Eye on Supplier Performance: Once a supplier is chosen and a contract is signed, it's crucial to monitor and manage their performance. Regularly check if they are meeting the terms of the agreement and take action if any issues arise.

Creating the Procurement Management Plan

  1. Define Procurement Goals: The first step in crafting a procurement management plan is to clearly outline the objectives of the procurement process. What specific goals are we aiming to achieve through procurement? Clarifying these objectives lays the groundwork for developing effective strategies.
  2. Formulate Procurement Strategies: Following the definition of objectives, the next crucial step involves devising strategies to attain them. What are the most streamlined and impactful approaches to procure the necessary goods and services? This phase requires thorough research and analysis to identify optimal options.
  3. Establish a Procurement Budget: Once objectives and strategies are in place, the subsequent step is creating a realistic procurement budget. This budget serves as the financial blueprint, covering the costs of all required goods and services. Ensuring the budget is both achievable and practical is essential to prevent issues down the road.
  4. Seek Bids from Suppliers: In the fourth stage, solicit bids from potential suppliers to secure the best prices for needed goods and services. Conducting a fair and transparent bidding process is vital to avoid any grievances from suppliers.
  5. Evaluate and Choose Suppliers: After receiving all bids, evaluate and select the most suitable supplier. The chosen supplier must fulfill all the outlined requirements in the procurement plan based on specific needs. Subsequently, negotiate and sign a contract with the selected supplier.
  6. Manage the Procurement Process: The sixth step involves actively managing the procurement process, ensuring timely and budget-friendly delivery of all goods and services. Monitoring supplier performance is also critical to ensure compliance with the contract's stipulations.
  7. Conclude the Procurement Process: The final step is to close out the procurement process, confirming that all payments are settled, and documents are appropriately filed. Following the completion of the procurement process, it becomes necessary to assess the results and make any required adjustments.

Fitting Procurements into an Agile Context

Agile procurement is a way of buying things that's more flexible and faster than the usual method. In this approach, some steps happen at the same time to save time, and the team checks how things are going regularly and makes changes if needed. Also, agile procurement teams are smaller than the regular ones, so they can agree on things quickly. Because it's all about being fast, teams using agile procurement use special software to make some steps automatic. This helps them analyze suppliers and what's happening in the market better. In a nutshell, they use technology to speed things up and be more efficient.
